\subsection{5.1 Quantitative
Overview}\label{quantitative-overview}

The quantitative results reveal clear performance differences among
the four reconstruction methods across the selected Dong drum tower
scenes. As shown in Fig. 7, 3DGS achieved the highest average PSNR and
SSIM values, reaching \textbf{31.60 dB} and \textbf{0.924},
respectively. This indicates that 3DGS provided the strongest overall
image fidelity and structural similarity under the current experimental
setting. In contrast, INGP achieved the lowest average LPIPS value of
\textbf{0.075}, suggesting superior perceptual similarity in terms of
deep-feature-based visual distance.

NeRF produced relatively stable but moderate results across the four
scenes, while 2DGS showed substantially weaker image-rendering
performance under the current configuration. It should be noted that
these quantitative results primarily reflect image-level rendering
quality. They do not, by themselves, fully represent geometric accuracy
or conservation usability. Therefore, the following sections further
interpret the results in relation to architectural readability and
conservation-relevant components.

As shown in Fig. 7, the quantitative results reveal clear differences
among the four reconstruction methods across the selected Dong drum
tower scenes. 3DGS achieved the strongest overall image fidelity and
structural consistency, obtaining the highest average PSNR and SSIM
values of 31.60 dB and 0.924, respectively. Its advantage was
particularly evident in PSNR, where it ranked first in all four scenes,
with the highest value observed in Chaoli at 33.53 dB; however, because
Chaoli does not include an independent test set under the current split,
this scene-specific result should be interpreted with caution. In terms
of structural similarity, 3DGS also obtained the best SSIM in three of
the four scenes, while Congjiang showed only a small difference between
3DGS and INGP, with SSIM values of 0.897 and 0.895, respectively. INGP
ranked second in average PSNR and achieved the best average LPIPS value
of 0.075, indicating strong perceptual similarity and texture-level
visual quality, particularly in the Congjiang and Zeli scenes. NeRF
produced relatively stable but moderate results, with PSNR values around
27 dB and an average SSIM of 0.893, while 2DGS showed consistently
weaker image-level performance under the present configuration. Overall,
3DGS provides the most reliable balance between rendering fidelity and
structural readability for high-fidelity documentation, whereas INGP
remains advantageous for perceptual visualization and efficient
reconstruction. These findings suggest that method performance should be
interpreted in relation to conservation-oriented tasks rather than as a
single universal ranking.

\subsection{5.2 Visual Comparison Across
Architectural
Components}\label{visual-comparison-across-architectural-components}

To complement the image-level metrics, visual comparison was
conducted using representative architectural views, including the base,
tower enclosure, main body, tower tip, and panoramic view. This analysis
helps translate quantitative rendering performance into
conservation-relevant interpretation by examining whether reconstructed
outputs preserve the readability of architectural components.

The visual comparison indicates that 3DGS produced the most
consistent reconstruction behavior across the examined architectural
views. Its outputs better preserved the overall tower silhouette,
layered roof organization, and continuity of dense timber components.
This is particularly important for Dong drum towers, where visual
readability depends on the clear separation of eave layers, roof-crown
details, and timber-frame boundaries. INGP often produced visually
competitive outputs and showed strong perceptual quality, especially in
texture-rich regions. NeRF generated relatively stable but smoother
renderings, with weaker recovery of high-frequency architectural
details. Under the current setting, 2DGS showed more visible instability
in color consistency, structural continuity, and local component
preservation.

To avoid overclaiming, the component-level assessment in Table 14 is
reported as a structured qualitative interpretation rather than an
independent quantitative measurement. The assessment is based on
representative rendered views and the observed consistency between
global quantitative performance and local architectural readability.

For interpretive consistency, qualitative grades were used as
heuristic labels: Excellent (PSNR \textgreater{} 30, SSIM \textgreater{}
0.90), Good (PSNR 27-30, SSIM 0.85-0.90), Moderate (PSNR 25-27, SSIM
0.80-0.85), and Poor (PSNR \textless{} 25, SSIM \textless{} 0.80). These
labels are not independent per-component measurements; rather, they
provide a structured interpretation of the quantitative and visual
results at the architectural-component level.

The component-level comparison supports the quantitative findings.
3DGS is more suitable for preserving architectural readability in
layered timber structures, particularly where eave hierarchy, roof-crown
contours, and timber-frame continuity are important. INGP remains
valuable for perceptually convincing visualization and efficient
reconstruction. NeRF can serve as a stable baseline, but it is less
effective in recovering fine architectural details. The weaker
image-level and visual performance of 2DGS under the present setting
should not be interpreted as a general failure of the method; rather, it
suggests that geometry-oriented evaluation metrics may be needed to
fully assess its potential for surface-based heritage documentation.

In summary, the quantitative and visual results indicate that 3DGS
provides the strongest overall reconstruction performance for the
selected Dong drum tower scenes in terms of PSNR, SSIM, and
component-level architectural readability. INGP achieves the best
average LPIPS and remains competitive for perceptual visualization and
efficient reconstruction. NeRF performs consistently but with moderate
fidelity and lower efficiency. 2DGS underperforms in the current
image-level rendering evaluation, but its surface-oriented formulation
remains relevant for future geometry-focused assessment.

These results provide the basis for the subsequent discussion, where
method performance is interpreted not only as an algorithmic ranking but
also in relation to conservation tasks such as high-fidelity
documentation, rapid field reconstruction, HBIM preparation, digital
exhibition, restoration survey, and periodic monitoring.

\subsection{5.3 Visual Comparison of Rendering
Results}\label{visual-comparison-of-rendering-results}

To complement the quantitative evaluation, visual comparisons were
conducted for the four Dong drum tower scenes, as shown in \textbf{Figs.
8--11}. For each scene, five representative architectural views were
selected, including the base, tower enclosure, main body, tower tip, and
panoramic view. The four compared methods---3DGS, 2DGS, INGP, and
NeRF---are arranged consistently in each figure to facilitate
cross-method and cross-scene comparison. This visual analysis is
intended to connect the numerical metrics with architecturally
meaningful features, such as multi-layered eaves, timber-frame
boundaries, roof contours, stone bases, tower tips, and shadowed
regions.

Across the four scenes, 3DGS consistently produced the strongest
overall visual realism and geometric coherence. Its advantage was most
evident in the reconstruction of multi-layered eaves, roof contours, and
repetitive timber-frame structures. INGP often generated sharper
perceptual textures, particularly in stone bases and other highly
textured surface regions. NeRF produced comparatively conservative
renderings: although its outputs showed lower detail sharpness, they
exhibited fewer severe structural failures than 2DGS. Under the current
experimental configuration, 2DGS showed noticeable color distortion,
texture blurring, and structural degradation in several scenes.

recovery but fewer severe distortions than 2DGS. Fourth, 2DGS does not
achieve satisfactory rendering quality under the current configuration;
its potential value should be interpreted more cautiously in relation to
future geometry-oriented evaluation rather than current visual-quality
metrics alone.

\section{\textbf{6.}
Discussion}\label{discussion}

\subsection{6.1 Conservation-oriented
interpretation}\label{conservation-oriented-interpretation}

The results should be interpreted in relation to conservation tasks
rather than as a single algorithmic ranking. For Dong drum towers,
useful reconstruction requires not only image fidelity but also the
readability of layered eaves, roof-crown systems, timber frameworks,
bracket-like supports, and shadowed internal spaces.

Under the present benchmark, 3DGS achieved the highest PSNR and SSIM,
indicating stronger image fidelity and structural consistency. It is
therefore more suitable for high-fidelity visual documentation and
structurally readable reconstruction. INGP achieved the lowest average
LPIPS and the shortest training time, making it more appropriate for
rapid reconstruction, preliminary visualization, and field-based
feedback. NeRF remained a stable but less efficient baseline. 2DGS
performed weakly under image-level metrics, but this does not exclude
its potential value for future geometry-oriented evaluation.

Architectural complexity also affected reconstruction behavior.
Differences in roof hierarchy, timber density, occlusion, shadow
distribution, and viewpoint continuity influenced method performance
across the four scenes. These results support a task-oriented
interpretation: 3DGS is preferable when structural readability is
required, whereas INGP is practical when fast and perceptually
acceptable reconstruction is prioritized.

\subsection{6.2 Implications for digital heritage
workflows}\label{implications-for-digital-heritage-workflows}

Three implications follow from these findings. First, PSNR, SSIM, and
LPIPS are useful but insufficient for heritage documentation; component
readability must also be considered. Second, method selection should
depend on the intended task. High-fidelity documentation, rapid
reconstruction, digital exhibition, HBIM preparation, restoration
survey, and monitoring require different balances of fidelity,
efficiency, structural readability, and geometric reliability. Third,
UAV-based neural reconstruction should complement, not replace,
established documentation workflows. Tasks involving measurement,
deformation analysis, structural diagnosis, or material condition
assessment still require geometric validation and expert interpretation.
